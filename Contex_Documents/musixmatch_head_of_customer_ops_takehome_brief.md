## PART A — The Brief (faithful reproduction, reorganized)

### A.1 The Goal

Design and build a **working prototype** of your vision for **AI-first customer operations**.

The core premise: **one person, equipped with the right AI tools and workflow design, should be able to handle what a traditional team of ten would require.** This is not a request to theorize — they want to see a prototype concept in action.

### A.2 What to Build

- Build a **working prototype** — at least **one practical use case running end-to-end**.
- It does **not** need to be polished, **but it needs to work**.
- Choose **the problem domain you know best**: the goal is **depth over breadth**, and real hands-on familiarity will show.
- **You decide the context.** What matters is that you're solving a **real problem you understand well, not a hypothetical one.**

**Your prototype should show three things:**

1. **A concrete workflow where AI handles volume autonomously** — routing, drafting, classifying, retrieving, or responding — **without a human touching every case.**
2. **The points where a human stays in the loop**, and your **reasoning for why those handoffs exist.**
3. **How the 1-person-doing-the-work-of-10 ratio actually holds**: what volume AI absorbs, what quality signals tell you it's working, and what breaks down at scale.

### A.3 The 1-to-10 Ratio — Be Specific

When they say *"1 person doing what would normally require 10,"* they mean it concretely:

> A traditional customer ops team of 10 handles **X** tickets/day, manages **Y** client relationships, responds within **Z** hours, and escalates **N%** of cases.

Your model should **show, not just claim**, how a single operator using AI can **match or exceed that throughput without sacrificing quality.**

In the presentation, **come prepared to answer:**
- Where exactly does the leverage come from?
- What tasks did AI absorb?
- What is the human actually doing with their time?

### A.4 Logistics

- You have **one week**.
- Timing is **flexible** — let them know what schedule works best for you.

### A.5 The 30-Minute Panel

- **Lead with the demo.** Walk them through a **live interaction**; show the system working.
- **Narrating a slide deck about your vision is NOT a substitute for showing the thing itself.**

**After the demo, walk them through:**
- The **design choices** you made and why.
- Where you'd take this with **more time, a full team, or production infrastructure.**
- What you'd **measure** to know it's actually delivering **10x leverage.**

### A.6 No Constraints on Tools or Stack

Use whatever you're most effective with — off-the-shelf AI products, no-code platforms, custom code, or any combination. **There is no preferred answer.**

**What they're evaluating:**
- Your **judgment about the human/AI boundary.**
- Your **ability to ship something real.**
- **How you think about operations at scale.**

## PART B — Formatter's Analytic Layer (ADDED — not in original)

### B.1 Deliverables checklist (what must exist by panel day)

- [ ] A **working** prototype — one end-to-end use case that actually runs (working > polished).
- [ ] A live **demo** ready to drive (this is the centerpiece, not the deck).
- [ ] A clear story of the **human/AI boundary**: what AI does autonomously vs. where a human is kept in the loop, with the reasoning.
- [ ] A concrete **1:10 leverage model** with defined numbers (see B.3).
- [ ] A short **"with more time / full team / production"** roadmap.
- [ ] A defined set of **metrics** that would prove 10x leverage.

### B.2 The four questions to have crisp answers for

1. Where does the leverage come from? (the mechanism, not a slogan)
2. What tasks did AI absorb? (named, specific)
3. What is the human now doing with their freed time? (the higher-value work)
4. What breaks down at scale, and what are the quality signals you'd watch?

### B.3 ⚠️ Placeholder warning (highest-value disambiguation)

In A.3, **`X`, `Y`, `Z`, and `N` are deliberate placeholders in the original brief — they are NOT data lost in transcription.** The brief is instructing the candidate to **define these concrete numbers themselves** for whatever domain they choose:

- `X` = tickets/day a 10-person team handles
- `Y` = client relationships managed
- `Z` = response-time SLA (hours)
- `N%` = escalation rate

Do not hunt for "the real values" and do not invent them as if they came from Musixmatch. They are the candidate's to model and defend.

### B.4 Format priority (explicit in the brief, easy to underweight)

The brief states twice, forcefully, that the **demo leads** and a slide deck is **not a substitute** for the working system. Practical implication for any prep work: **build the working thing first; the deck is a thin support layer around the live demo, not the main artifact.**

### B.5 Evaluation rubric (what "good" looks like to them)

| Dimension | What they're actually judging |
|---|---|
| Human/AI boundary judgment | Are the autonomy vs. handoff decisions sound and well-reasoned? |
| Ability to ship | Did you build something **real that works**, not a mockup? |
| Operations-at-scale thinking | Do you understand throughput, quality signals, and failure modes at volume? |
| Leverage proof | Is the 1:10 ratio **demonstrated with numbers**, not asserted? |

### B.6 Domain-choice note

The brief leaves the **problem domain fully open** and explicitly rewards picking the one the candidate knows best ("depth over breadth"). It does **not** prescribe a specific vertical. Any domain selection is a candidate decision, not a requirement of the brief.
